About Me

I'm a Cognitive Science graduate from Simon Fraser University with a deep interest in why people make decisions — and how those decisions shape the products they use. My background spans UX research, interaction design, and behavioral science.

Most recently, I completed a co-op as a User Researcher at Kabam, a mobile gaming studio in Vancouver, where I ran end-to-end research for multiple live game titles — from large-scale surveys to moderated playtests. I synthesized player behavior insights and built stakeholder-facing reports that influenced real product decisions.

"Good research doesn't just tell you what users do — it tells you why, and what to do about it."

Before Kabam, I spent two years at Vantedge as a Project Designer, and completed a bootcamp in Applied UX Design at SAIT. I've also worked as a Research Assistant in the Spalek Lab at SFU, where I gained hands-on experience with EEG and eye-tracking equipment to study cognitive processes.

I'm now actively seeking full-time roles in UX Research or UX Design at organizations building technology that makes a genuine difference — teams like those at Microsoft, Google, or Meta.

Overview

Understanding what keeps players engaged

At Kabam, I joined the UX research team to help answer some of the most pressing questions in mobile gaming: Why do players churn? What drives session length? How do new feature releases land with different player segments?

I was responsible for conducting end-to-end research — from designing survey instruments and recruiting participants, all the way to synthesizing findings and presenting to product and design stakeholders.

Process

Research Methods & Execution

Survey Design & DeploymentDesigned large-scale surveys targeting specific player segments, optimizing question flow to reduce drop-off while capturing actionable behavioral data.
Moderated PlaytestsLed moderated playtest sessions — both remote via Zoom and in-person — observing player behavior in real time and probing motivations with follow-up questions.
Metric AnalysisCalculated NPS and other key performance metrics, synthesizing quantitative patterns against qualitative interview themes to build a complete picture of player sentiment.
Stakeholder ReportingTranslated raw data into polished, narrative-driven reports and presentation decks tailored to product managers, designers, and senior stakeholders.
"Players don't churn because the game is hard. They churn when they can't see the path forward — when progress feels invisible."
Impact

Research that moves the needle

My research insights were directly incorporated into design briefs and product roadmaps. By uncovering specific friction points in the new player onboarding experience, the team was able to prioritize UI changes that reduced early-session drop-off.

The participant recruitment and session management systems I introduced also reduced scheduling overhead and improved no-show rates across ongoing studies.

Centro —
Budgeting App

A 4-week UX design project for a social budgeting startup. My role was to help translate complex financial data into a visually engaging experience for young adults.

RoleUX Designer
Duration4 weeks
Team2 UX, 2 PM
ToolsFigma, Miro, Adobe
The Problem

Budgeting feels like a punishment

Centro came to us with a clear challenge: young adults know they should budget, but they don't. Not because they lack the tools, but because the experience of reviewing finances feels clinical, judgmental, and disconnected from their actual lives.

Our persona, Samara — a 32-year-old communications analyst saving for a downpayment — summed it up: "Budgeting shouldn't be fun, it should be less painful."

Research

21 interviews. 33 surveys. Real insights.

We followed a design thinking approach, with an intense emphasis on the discovery phase. To build a rich picture of people's relationships with money, our team conducted 21 interviews and surveyed 33 individuals, building on Centro's existing research data.

We also conducted competitive analysis of budgeting and behavioral apps, identifying a clear gap: no app was translating financial data into a narrative people actually cared about.

The insight: visualizing the impact of decisions on future financial scenarios was more motivating than tracking past spending.
Solution

Financial storytelling, not financial tracking

We hypothesized that if we provided a visually captivating platform that translated spending habits into future financial scenarios, we could empower individuals to see their financial future clearly.

Inspired by Spotify Wrapped and Instagram Stories, we designed a swipeable monthly recap experience — the "November Story" — that turns spending data into a personal narrative. Combined with uplifting, context-aware push notifications, we aimed to make opening the app feel rewarding rather than anxiety-inducing.

Testing

14 participants. Iterative refinement.

Usability testing with 14 participants surfaced key friction points: confusion around the term "Overview," a preference for income and expenses to appear together, and a desire for swiping interactions. We iterated from low-fidelity to mid-fidelity, incorporating all feedback and adding the Category Spend feature and an animated goal vision board.

Key Features Designed

  • Monthly "Story" recap (Spotify Wrapped-style)
  • Swipeable overview interface
  • Category spend breakdown
  • Goal vision board with animations
  • Uplifting push notification system

Methods Used

  • 21 semi-structured interviews
  • 33 surveys
  • Competitive analysis
  • Persona development
  • Low–mid fidelity prototyping
  • 14-person usability test

Gong Cha —
App Redesign

A 3-week UX redesign project focused on improving usability, information architecture, and visual design for Gong Cha's mobile ordering app.

RoleUX Designer
Duration3 weeks
TypeApp Redesign
ToolsFigma, Miro, Adobe
The Problem

A cluttered menu nobody wanted to navigate

The existing Gong Cha app had a reputation problem. Users could eventually find what they needed, but the journey was painful — a cluttered and lengthy menu with no drink descriptions, no nutritional information, an outdated visual design, and a frustrating sign-up process.

As one interviewee put it: "If I was ordering for the first time, I would need more descriptions and pictures... maybe a carousel menu at the top so I don't have to scroll so much."

Research

Surveys, interviews, and competitive analysis

Research with 12 participants aged 18–55 highlighted consistent pain points: inability to order within the app, a cluttered menu, missing drink descriptions, no nutritional info, overwhelming choices, outdated interface, and a lengthy sign-up process.

Competitive analysis of Coco Fresh Tea and Starbucks revealed what best-in-class looked like: organized menus, delivery options, seasonal item spotlights, and detailed drink descriptions with imagery.

The gap: Gong Cha had the product quality — what it lacked was an app experience worthy of it.
Solution

Clarity, speed, and visual delight

Restructured Menu ArchitectureIntroduced a responsive carousel menu at the top for browsing by category — reducing cognitive load and eliminating unnecessary scrolling.
Rich Drink DescriptionsAdded ingredient details, flavor profiles, and size imagery to every drink item, empowering first-time users to make confident choices.
Modern Visual OverhaulRedesigned the app with Gong Cha's brand-aligned palette — clean, warm, and premium — replacing the cluttered legacy UI with a high-contrast, image-forward layout.
Streamlined CheckoutSimplified the order and checkout flow, reducing the number of steps to place an order and adding a prominent delivery vs. pickup toggle up front.
Testing

3-person usability test. High success rates.

Usability testing with participants aged 18–50 showed high success rates on core tasks: selecting a drink, customizing it, adding to cart, and checkout. The only task with a 50% success rate was editing an existing order — flagged as a clear improvement area. Feedback highlighted the need for more drink detail and a quantity adjustment option.

Deliverables

  • Competitive analysis
  • User personas (Sarah, 22)
  • Customer journey map
  • Wireframes (2 concepts)
  • Interactive prototype
  • Usability test report

Key Wins

  • Carousel navigation reduced scroll fatigue
  • Rich descriptions improved first-time ordering confidence
  • High usability scores on core flows

Next Steps

Improve the order-editing flow (50% success rate), add quantity adjustment in cart, and conduct a second round of testing with first-time Gong Cha users.
