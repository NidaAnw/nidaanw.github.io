01 — About

The person
behind the research

I'm a Cognitive Science graduate from Simon Fraser University with a deep fascination for how the brain shapes the way we interact with technology. My work lives at the intersection of behavioural research, neuroscience, and design — and I believe the most impactful products are built by people who genuinely understand why humans do what they do.

Most recently, I completed a co-op as a User Researcher at Kabam, a Vancouver-based mobile gaming studio, where I ran end-to-end research for multiple live game titles. Before that, I spent time as a Research Assistant in the Spalek Lab at SFU, working directly with EEG and eye-tracking tools to study attention and cognitive processes.

I apply neuroscience and psychology principles as a compass in every project I take on. It grounds the work in how people actually think and feel — making it more honest, more human, and more likely to create real change.

"I use neuroscience and psychology as my design compass. It makes the work more honest — more human."

I'm drawn to work with real stakes. That's why neurotechnology and BCI feel like the most exciting frontier I can imagine — and why I'll be starting my MSc in Neuroscience at SFU in 2026. The brain is the most complex interface ever built, and I want to design for it.

Overview

Understanding what keeps players coming back

At Kabam I joined the UX research team to investigate pressing questions in mobile gaming: why do players churn? What drives session length? How do new feature releases land across different player segments? I owned end-to-end research — from designing instruments and recruiting participants through to synthesizing findings and presenting to stakeholders.

Process

Four research pillars

1
Survey Design & Deployment

Large-scale survey instruments targeting specific player segments, balancing depth with completion rates.

2
Moderated Playtests

Remote and in-person sessions observing behaviour in real time, probing motivation through follow-up questions.

3
Metric Analysis

NPS calculation and quantitative synthesis against qualitative themes to build a complete picture of player sentiment.

4
Stakeholder Reporting

Narrative-driven reports and presentation decks tailored for PMs, designers, and senior leadership.

"Players don't churn because the game is hard. They churn when they can't see the path forward — when progress feels invisible."— Key insight from playtest synthesis
Impact

Research that moved the roadmap

My insights were directly incorporated into design briefs, with specific friction points in new player onboarding prioritized for UI changes. Recruitment systems I introduced improved session scheduling efficiency across ongoing studies.

Deliverables

  • Multi-wave survey instruments
  • Moderated playtest protocols
  • NPS analysis reports
  • Executive stakeholder decks
  • Participant recruitment system

Skills Applied

  • Quant & qual synthesis
  • Participant recruitment
  • Behavioural observation
  • Data analysis (Excel)
  • Stakeholder communication

Context

With tens of millions of players across their titles, research at Kabam operates at genuine scale. Every insight had measurable downstream effects on engagement and retention.

Centro — Budgeting App
Redesigning how Gen-Z relates to money — using Spotify Wrapped-style storytelling and psychology-backed behavioural nudges to make financial literacy feel less painful.
4 Weeks · 2022→
CentroBudgeting App

A 4-week sprint for a social budgeting startup. My challenge: make a 32-year-old woman actually want to open her budgeting app. The insight that drove everything came from behavioural psychology.

RoleUX Designer
Duration4 weeks
Team2 UX · 2 PM
ToolsFigma · Miro · Adobe
The Problem

Budgeting apps treat anxiety as a feature

The existing budgeting landscape is built around guilt: red numbers, overspending alerts, clinical charts. It's optimized for accountants, not humans. Our persona Samara — a 32-year-old communications analyst saving for a downpayment — said it best:

"Budgeting shouldn't be fun, it should be less painful."— Samara, primary persona
Research

21 interviews. 33 surveys. One core insight.

We followed a design thinking approach with a heavy emphasis on the empathy phase. Using principles from cognitive science, I identified the key gap: people are motivated by prospective thinking — imagining their future self — far more than reviewing their past. No existing budgeting app was leveraging this.

Solution

Financial storytelling, not financial tracking

Inspired by Spotify Wrapped and Instagram Stories — and grounded in behavioural psychology — we designed a swipeable monthly "Story" that transforms spending data into a personal narrative. Paired with uplifting, context-aware push notifications, the experience rewards opening the app rather than penalising it.

Testing

14 participants. Iterated twice.

Usability testing surfaced key friction points: terminology confusion, a preference for income and expenses together, and desire for swipe gestures. We iterated from low to mid fidelity, adding a Category Spend feature and animated goal vision board.

Psychology Applied

This project is where I first consciously applied psychological principles — specifically prospect theory and temporal motivation theory — to design decisions. Visualizing your future financial self is more motivating than reviewing past spending. It's the same instinct that drives my fascination with BCI and neurotechnology.

Features Designed

  • Monthly "Story" recap (swipeable)
  • Income + expense overview
  • Category spend breakdown
  • Animated goal vision board
  • Uplifting push notifications

Methods

  • 21 semi-structured interviews
  • 33 surveys
  • Competitive analysis
  • Persona development
  • Low–mid fidelity prototyping
  • 14-person usability test

Psychology Applied

  • Prospect theory
  • Temporal motivation theory
  • Positive reinforcement design
  • Habit loop framework

Gong ChaApp Redesign

A 3-week redesign project improving usability, information architecture, and visual language for Gong Cha's mobile ordering app. A personal one — I love bubble tea.

RoleUX Designer
Duration3 weeks
TypeApp Redesign
ToolsFigma · Miro · Adobe
The Problem

A great product trapped in a confusing app

Gong Cha's product quality is undeniable — but their app was a cluttered, scrolling menu with no descriptions, no images, no nutritional info, and a frustrating sign-up wall. First-time users were essentially guessing.

"If I was ordering for the first time, I would need more descriptions and pictures. Maybe a carousel so I don't have to scroll so much."— Interview participant
Research

12 participants. Competitive benchmarking.

Survey and interview data from 12 participants (ages 18–55) revealed consistent pain points. Competitive analysis of Coco Fresh Tea and Starbucks showed what best-in-class mobile ordering could look like.

Solution

Clarity, speed, visual delight

1
Carousel Navigation

Category-based carousel reducing cognitive load and eliminating endless scrolling.

2
Rich Drink Cards

Ingredient details, flavor descriptions, and size imagery empowering first-time users.

3
Visual Overhaul

Clean, warm, image-forward redesign aligned with Gong Cha's brand identity.

4
Streamlined Checkout

Simplified order flow with prominent delivery vs. pickup toggle upfront.

Deliverables

  • Competitive analysis report
  • User persona (Sarah, 22)
  • Customer journey map
  • 2 wireframe concepts
  • Interactive prototype
  • Usability test report

Key Wins

  • Carousel reduced scroll fatigue
  • Descriptions improved ordering confidence
  • High usability scores on core flows

Next Steps

Improve the order-editing flow (50% success rate), add cart quantity adjustment, and run a second round with first-time users specifically.
